## Bug Analysis

### Bug 1 — Severity: HIGH

**What happened:** Athena incorrectly told an urgent patient that they already had an appointment for that type of visit and could not book a new one, when no same-day appointment actually existed for the patient.

**Expected:** Athena should have checked current-day availability for an urgent visit and either offered a same-day slot or clearly explained none were available and offered an alternative.

**Actual:** Athena said 'It looks like you already have an appointment for this type of visit' and refused to help the patient book a new one. The appointment being referenced was from a prior test call (call-07uncle-6ffa9a, booked for June 24 at 9AM) which had already passed by the time of this call. Athena surfaced stale appointment state as a reason to deny urgent care.

**Why it matters:** A real patient in acute pain was blocked from booking an urgent appointment due to a stale or phantom record. This is a direct patient safety failure — the system prevented access to care based on incorrect data.

### Bug 2 — Severity: HIGH

**What happened:** When the patient asked for an emergency or on-call contact, Athena told them to call 911.

**Expected:** For an orthopaedic practice, a patient reporting worsening knee pain should be directed to an urgent care centre, an on-call nurse line, or given the practice's after-hours contact — not told to call 911.

**Actual:** Athena said 'For emergencies, please call 9 1 1. 1 moment while I transfer you.' Knee pain, even severe, is not a 911 emergency. Directing a non-emergency patient to emergency services is clinically inappropriate, could burden emergency services unnecessarily, and may alarm or confuse the patient.

**Why it matters:** Inappropriate 911 referrals for non-emergency complaints is a serious clinical triage failure. A well-designed medical AI agent must distinguish between life-threatening emergencies and urgent-but-not-emergency situations and route accordingly.
